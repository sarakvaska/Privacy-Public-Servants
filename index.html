Privacy and Public Servants

Should Public Servants Have the Right to

Disappear?

Overview


Personally Identifiable Information (PII) is defined as "any data that could potentially be used to identify a particular person."1 Examples of PII include a person's full name, date of birth, house address, and contact information, among others.

Much of this information for any individual can be found on the Internet, through online directory services, data collection agencies, federal websites, and social media.

When it comes to privacy, many people are wary of their PII being freely available online. For some, however, this information being publicly available can be dangerous.

In this project, we're going to examine how public servants (including politicians, judges, prosecutors, and law enforcement officers) should have the right to shield their PII from online directories and internet searches—as if they're disappearing from the internet.

Background


This idea of disappearing from the internet isn't a new one. The Right to Erasure is encoded in the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) in Article 17 as “The data subject shall have the right to obtain from the controller the erasure of personal data concerning him or her without undue delay and the controller shall have the obligation to erase personal data without undue delay”2 if one of a number of conditions applies, as seen on the right. The left column lists all the conditions where the right to erasure applies; the right column lists the conditions where the right to erasure doesn't apply.

The problem, however, is that 1) There is no universal legislation in the United States when it comes to Privacy or data protection online, 2) There are no rights to erasure, and 3) Personal information is online and often easily accessible. Not only is this creepy, but it can pose a risk to many—especially public servants.

So, how can we address this?

Why This Matters.


Although it may seem that this information being online is likely harmless, the case of Esther Salas proves that it can be very much not. Her PII being freely available online made it easier for Roy Den Hollander to kill her son and seriously injure her husband.

In the case of Caleb Hanna, what does it mean to show that so much of his PII is available online? For one, he seems not be worried about his privacy considering all of the information he puts out himself through so much of his social media activity.

The problem in these cases is that it shouldn't be possible to find someone's information online and harm them or their families, especially if the nature of their job is what motivated the crime. As we saw, Caleb Hanna is a controversial figure in the public eye. It wouldn't be surprising to know people aren't a fan of his. Esther Salas, meanwhile, is a judge whose job it is to make hard decisions, ones that may acquire her enemies.

By nature, the job of being a public servant (including judges, prosecutors, police officers, and elected officials) can be divisive positions. There will always be those who don't like certain policies, decisions, or actions. It shouldn't be the case that public servants should be subject to violence or harm because their PII was listed online and couldn't be removed.

There should be nationwide legislation allowing public servants to shield their PII from internet searches and online directories.

Current Legislation


There currently exist no federal-level privacy laws. States can choose to create their own legislation.

On a company level, there aren't even federal laws requiring companies to have privacy policies, unless they collect information about children, are engaged in financial activities, or are a health service.


However, on November 20, 2020, New Jersey Governor Murphy signed “Daniel’s Law,” a piece of legislation introduced by the NJ U.S. senators which shields personally identifiable information (such as home addresses, telephone numbers, photos of their homes and cars) of federal judges, prosecutors, law enforcement officers, and their immediate family from public disclosure online.

According to Daniel's Law:

  • Government agencies, individuals and businesses are prohibited from knowingly publishing on the internet, or otherwise making available, the home address or unpublished home telephone number of any active or retired judge or any active or retired prosecutor.4
  • Any active or retired judge, prosecutor, or law enforcement officer whose home address or unpublished telephone number is disclosed on the internet or otherwise made available to the public, or whose immediate family member’s name, home address, or unpublished phone number is disclosed on the internet or otherwise made available to the public, can request that the information be removed. The government agency, individual or business would be required to remove the information within 72 hours of receiving such a request in writing.5

Policy


While Daniel's law allows any active or retired judges, prosecutors or law enforcement officers to shield their PII or request its removal, the law doesn't apply to elected officials. It is also only active in the state of New Jersey. Our policy will change that.

Public Servants play vital roles in keeping the public safe, and in doing so, they often jeopardize their own safety, becoming targets of vengeful criminals or litigants. We need to protect them as they protect all of us. Our policy will help to ensure the privacy and security of these public servants and their families.

We're proposing a national policy that will:

  1. Allow public servants to request the removal of their or their family's personally identifiable information from online directories and searches.
  2. Require companies to perform the removal within 72 hours of request.
  3. Exist in conjunction with a national Oversight Committee who will oversee requests for removal and manage cases.
  4. Require step-by-step records of process from the oversight committee to ensure that records of the decision process and the reasons for the decision are made and preserved.
  5. Require all public servants to be educated on their privacy rights, including their right to removal.

If you would like to read our full policy proposal, including our reasoning behind each requirement, visit Policy on the Right to Shield Personally Identifiable Information as a Public Servant.

Conclusion


With this project, our aim was to explore the publicly available information of public servants, conduct a case study to determine how easy it is to find out the most personal information about someone (including their phone number and address), and write a policy that could serve as a nationwide framework to better protect public servants.


We were motivated to choose this as a final project because we're living in a time of rising hate crimes and increasing personal attacks. While we may not be able to stop hate, action can be taken to better protect the women and men who serve the public.

No person who takes on the responsibility of serving as a federal judge, police officer, elected official, prosecutor, or otherwise, should ever have to live in fear that they or their family could be targeted by someone who is able to easily access their personally identifiable information.

The goal of our project and policy is to better protect the privacy of public servants by prohibiting that their personally identifiable information be shared without their consent and allowing them to remove it from online directories and searches at their request.
